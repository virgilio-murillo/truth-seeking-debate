# OPENING STATEMENT — TEAM A
## In Favor: The Doctrine of Eternal Security (Perseverance of the Saints) Is True

---

## I. THESIS

We affirm that the doctrine commonly called "Once Saved, Always Saved" — more precisely articulated as the *perseverance of the saints* — is true when properly understood: those whom God has genuinely regenerated and sealed with the Holy Spirit will infallibly persevere to final salvation, not by the strength of their own will, but by the irrevocable commitment of God expressed through the ἀρραβών (arrabon) of the Spirit. The metaphor of the *arrabon* does not merely *suggest* but *entails* unconditional divine guarantee for those who are truly in Christ.

We distinguish at the outset between two formulations:
- **Vulgar "OSAS"**: A person who once prayed a prayer is saved regardless of subsequent total apostasy — *this we do not defend*.
- **Perseverance of the Saints**: Those whom God has truly regenerated will, by the power of the Spirit's indwelling guarantee, persevere in faith unto the end — *this we affirm*.

---

## II. THE ἀρραβών TEXTS: GREEK EXEGESIS

### A. The Three Occurrences

The noun **ἀρραβών** (G728) appears only three times in the NT, all in Paul:

**2 Corinthians 1:21–22:**
> ὁ δὲ **βεβαιῶν** ἡμᾶς σὺν ὑμῖν εἰς Χριστὸν καὶ **χρίσας** ἡμᾶς θεός, ὁ καὶ **σφραγισάμενος** ἡμᾶς καὶ **δοὺς τὸν ἀρραβῶνα** τοῦ πνεύματος ἐν ταῖς καρδίαις ἡμῶν.

**2 Corinthians 5:5:**
> ὁ δὲ **κατεργασάμενος** ἡμᾶς εἰς αὐτὸ τοῦτο **θεός**, ὁ δοὺς ἡμῖν **τὸν ἀρραβῶνα τοῦ πνεύματος**.

**Ephesians 1:13–14:**
> ἐν ᾧ καὶ ὑμεῖς ἀκούσαντες τὸν λόγον τῆς ἀληθείας... ἐν ᾧ καὶ **πιστεύσαντες ἐσφραγίσθητε** τῷ πνεύματι τῆς ἐπαγγελίας τῷ ἁγίῳ, **ὅ ἐστιν ἀρραβὼν τῆς κληρονομίας ἡμῶν**, εἰς ἀπολύτρωσιν τῆς περιποιήσεως, εἰς ἔπαινον τῆς δόξης αὐτοῦ.

### B. Lexical-Commercial Background

The word ἀρραβών is a Semitic loanword (from Hebrew עֵרָבוֹן, cf. Gen 38:17–18 LXX) that entered Greek commercial vocabulary. Its technical meaning in Hellenistic papyri and legal documents is precise: **a down-payment that legally obligates the buyer to complete the full purchase**. It is not a conditional offer; it is a binding first installment that guarantees the remainder.

Key lexical data:
- **Strong's G728**: "a pledge, i.e. part of the purchase-money or property given in advance as security for the rest"
- In papyri (P.Oxy., BGU), the ἀρραβών creates a **legally binding obligation** — if the buyer fails to complete the purchase, the seller keeps the earnest; if the seller fails to deliver, they must return double. The metaphor places **God as the buyer** who has already paid the first installment.

The theological implication is devastating for conditionalism: **God is the one who has given the ἀρραβών**. If the ἀρραβών can fail to reach its consummation, then God has defaulted on His own commercial pledge. The metaphor's force depends entirely on the certainty of completion.

### C. The Semantic Cluster: σφραγίζω + βεβαιόω + ἀρραβών

In 2 Corinthians 1:21–22, Paul stacks four divine actions in a single sentence:

1. **βεβαιῶν** (V-PAP-NSM from βεβαιόω) — "the one confirming/establishing" — a legal term for guaranteeing a contract
2. **χρίσας** (V-AAP-NSM) — "having anointed" — consecration language
3. **σφραγισάμενος** (V-AMP-NSM from σφραγίζω) — "having sealed" — marking ownership and protection
4. **δοὺς τὸν ἀρραβῶνα** — "having given the earnest/guarantee"

The verb **σφραγίζω** (G4972) means "to stamp with a signet or private mark for security or preservation." In antiquity, a seal (σφραγίς) indicated: (a) ownership, (b) authenticity, and (c) protection from tampering. This is reinforced in Ephesians 4:30:

> καὶ μὴ λυπεῖτε τὸ πνεῦμα τὸ ἅγιον τοῦ θεοῦ, **ἐν ᾧ ἐσφραγίσθητε εἰς ἡμέραν ἀπολυτρώσεως**.

The seal is explicitly **εἰς ἡμέραν ἀπολυτρώσεως** — "unto the day of redemption." The terminus is fixed and future. The seal is not described as breakable or conditional; it endures *until* the eschatological consummation.

### D. The Grammatical Agent: God as Subject

A critical observation across all three ἀρραβών texts: **God is always the grammatical subject performing the action**. The participles are all nominative masculine singular referring to θεός:

- 2 Cor 1:22: ὁ... σφραγισάμενος... καὶ δοὺς — "the one who sealed... and gave"
- 2 Cor 5:5: ὁ... κατεργασάμενος... θεός, ὁ δοὺς — "God, the one who worked... who gave"
- Eph 1:13: ἐσφραγίσθητε (divine passive) — "you were sealed" (by God)

The security of the ἀρραβών rests not on human faithfulness but on **divine action and divine character**. The question is not "Can the believer break the seal?" but "Can God fail to honor His own guarantee?"

## III. THE BROADER PAULINE FRAMEWORK

### A. Romans 8:29–30 — The Golden Chain

> οὓς **προέγνω**, καὶ **προώρισεν**... οὓς δὲ προώρισεν, τούτους καὶ **ἐκάλεσεν**· καὶ οὓς ἐκάλεσεν, τούτους καὶ **ἐδικαίωσεν**· οὓς δὲ ἐδικαίωσεν, τούτους καὶ **ἐδόξασεν**.

The aorist tense of ἐδόξασεν (glorified) is theologically stunning — Paul speaks of glorification as *already accomplished* from God's perspective. The chain is unbroken: every link has the same referent (τούτους... τούτους... τούτους). There is no attrition between calling and glorification. Augustine commented on this passage:

> "God elected believers in order that they might believe, not because they already believed." (*De Praedestinatione Sanctorum*)

### B. Romans 8:35–39 — The Inseparability Declaration

> τίς ἡμᾶς **χωρίσει** ἀπὸ τῆς ἀγάπης τοῦ Χριστοῦ;... οὔτε ὕψωμα οὔτε βάθος **οὔτε τις κτίσις ἑτέρα δυνήσεται** ἡμᾶς χωρίσαι ἀπὸ τῆς ἀγάπης τοῦ θεοῦ τῆς ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ τῷ κυρίῳ ἡμῶν.

The phrase **οὔτε τις κτίσις ἑτέρα** ("nor any other created thing") is comprehensive. Since the believer is himself a κτίσις (creature), even the believer's own will cannot separate him from God's love. The verb δυνήσεται (future indicative of δύναμαι) expresses absolute inability, not mere improbability.

### C. Romans 11:29 — The Irrevocability Principle

> **ἀμεταμέλητα** γὰρ τὰ χαρίσματα καὶ ἡ κλῆσις τοῦ θεοῦ.

The adjective ἀμεταμέλητα (from ἀ-μεταμέλομαι, "without regret/change of mind") declares that God's gifts and calling are **irrevocable**. If the gift of the Spirit (the ἀρραβών) is among God's χαρίσματα, then by Paul's own principle it cannot be withdrawn.

### D. Philippians 1:6

> πεποιθὼς αὐτὸ τοῦτο ὅτι ὁ **ἐναρξάμενος** ἐν ὑμῖν ἔργον ἀγαθὸν **ἐπιτελέσει** ἄχρι ἡμέρας Χριστοῦ Ἰησοῦ·

The one who *began* (ἐναρξάμενος) the good work *will complete* (ἐπιτελέσει, future indicative) it. The subject is God, not the believer. The completion is stated as certainty, not possibility.

## IV. THE JOHANNINE WITNESS

### A. John 10:27–30

> τὰ πρόβατα τὰ ἐμὰ τῆς φωνῆς μου ἀκούουσιν... κἀγὼ δίδωμι αὐτοῖς **ζωὴν αἰώνιον**, καὶ **οὐ μὴ ἀπόλωνται εἰς τὸν αἰῶνα**, καὶ **οὐχ ἁρπάσει τις** αὐτὰ ἐκ τῆς χειρός μου. ὁ πατήρ μου... πάντων μεῖζων ἐστίν, καὶ **οὐδεὶς δύναται ἁρπάζειν** ἐκ τῆς χειρὸς τοῦ πατρός.

The double negative **οὐ μή** + aorist subjunctive (ἀπόλωνται) is the strongest form of negation in Greek — an emphatic denial of any possibility. The sheep are held in *two hands* — the Son's and the Father's — and "no one" (οὐδείς) can snatch them out.

### B. John 6:37–40

> πᾶν ὃ δίδωσίν μοι ὁ πατὴρ πρὸς ἐμὲ ἥξει... τοῦτο δέ ἐστιν τὸ θέλημα τοῦ πέμψαντός με ἵνα πᾶν ὃ δέδωκέν μοι **μὴ ἀπολέσω** ἐξ αὐτοῦ ἀλλὰ **ἀναστήσω** αὐτὸ τῇ ἐσχάτῃ ἡμέρᾳ.

Jesus declares it is the Father's will that He lose *nothing* (μὴ ἀπολέσω) of what has been given to Him. If a genuinely regenerate person could be finally lost, Christ would have failed to accomplish the Father's will — a theological impossibility.

### C. 1 John 2:19 — The Diagnostic Principle

> ἐξ ἡμῶν ἐξῆλθαν, ἀλλ' **οὐκ ἦσαν ἐξ ἡμῶν**· εἰ γὰρ ἐξ ἡμῶν ἦσαν, **μεμενήκεισαν ἂν** μεθ' ἡμῶν· ἀλλ' ἵνα φανερωθῶσιν ὅτι οὐκ εἰσὶν πάντες ἐξ ἡμῶν.

John provides the hermeneutical key for apparent counter-examples: those who depart were never genuinely "of us." The pluperfect + ἄν (μεμενήκεισαν ἂν) is a contrary-to-fact condition — "they would have remained" (but they didn't, proving they never truly belonged). Apostasy reveals false profession, not loss of genuine salvation.

## V. PETRINE CONFIRMATION

### A. 1 Peter 1:3–5

> ὁ... ἀναγεννήσας ἡμᾶς εἰς ἐλπίδα ζῶσαν... εἰς κληρονομίαν ἄφθαρτον καὶ ἀμίαντον καὶ ἀμάραντον, **τετηρημένην ἐν οὐρανοῖς** εἰς ὑμᾶς τοὺς ἐν **δυνάμει θεοῦ φρουρουμένους** διὰ πίστεως εἰς σωτηρίαν ἑτοίμην ἀποκαλυφθῆναι ἐν καιρῷ ἐσχάτῳ.

Two perfect participles describe the security:
- **τετηρημένην** (perfect passive of τηρέω) — the inheritance "has been kept and remains kept" in heaven
- **φρουρουμένους** (present passive of φρουρέω) — believers are "being guarded" by God's power

The instrument is **διὰ πίστεως** — "through faith." But this faith itself is sustained by the δύναμις θεοῦ (God's power). The security is not faith *alone* as a human achievement, but faith *as sustained by divine power*.

### B. Jude 24–25

> Τῷ δὲ **δυναμένῳ φυλάξαι** ὑμᾶς **ἀπταίστους** καὶ στῆσαι κατενώπιον τῆς δόξης αὐτοῦ ἀμώμους ἐν ἀγαλλιάσει

God is "able to keep you from stumbling" (φυλάξαι ἀπταίστους). The doxology attributes the preservation entirely to divine ability.

## VI. PATRISTIC SUPPORT

### A. John Chrysostom on Ephesians 1:14 (Homily 2, PG 62)

Chrysostom's commentary on the ἀρραβών is instructive. He writes:

> "He has purchased what we are most concerned in, our salvation; and has given us an earnest in the mean while. Why then did He not give the whole at once? Because neither have we, on our part, done the whole of our work. We have believed. This is a beginning; and He too on His part has given an earnest. When we show our faith by our works, then He will add the rest."

Chrysostom also explains the purpose of the ἀρραβών language:

> "For this reason he further calls it an earnest... for an earnest is a part of the whole."

And on the phrase "unto the praise of His glory":

> "Were it for our sake only, he means to say, that God did this, there might be some room for misgiving. But if it be for His own sake, and in order to display His goodness, he assigns, as a sort of witness, a reason why these things never possibly could be otherwise."

This last statement is crucial: Chrysostom argues that because God's own glory is at stake in the completion of salvation, "these things never possibly could be otherwise." The guarantee is grounded in God's self-interest in His own δόξα.

### B. Augustine — *De Dono Perseverantiae* (429 AD)

Augustine's mature theology explicitly teaches that perseverance is a divine gift given to the elect:

> "When perseverance is granted to a person, he cannot but persevere." (*De Dono Perseverantiae*, ch. 9)

Augustine distinguished between the *donum perseverantiae* (gift of perseverance) given to the elect and the general grace given to all baptized. His position: those who truly have the ἀρραβών of the Spirit — the elect — will infallibly persevere, because God's gift is irrevocable.

On Romans 8:30, Augustine wrote:

> "God elected believers in order that they might believe, not because they already believed." (*De Praedestinatione Sanctorum*)

### C. 2 Timothy 2:19 — The Patristic Seal

> ὁ μέντοι στερεὸς θεμέλιος τοῦ θεοῦ ἕστηκεν, ἔχων τὴν **σφραγῖδα** ταύτην· **Ἔγνω κύριος τοὺς ὄντας αὐτοῦ**

"The firm foundation of God stands, having this seal: 'The Lord knows those who are His.'" The σφραγίς (seal) here is God's knowledge of His own — an unshakeable divine cognition that cannot err or be frustrated.

## VII. SYSTEMATIC-THEOLOGICAL ARGUMENT

### A. The Nature of Eternal Life

If ζωὴ αἰώνιος (John 10:28) can be lost, it was never truly αἰώνιος. The adjective αἰώνιος, when applied to divine realities, denotes not merely "long-lasting" but "pertaining to the age to come" — qualitatively different, eschatological life. A "temporary eternal life" is an oxymoron.

### B. The Trinitarian Structure of Salvation

The ἀρραβών texts reveal a Trinitarian economy:
- The **Father** purposes and predestines (Eph 1:11)
- The **Son** accomplishes redemption (Eph 1:7)
- The **Spirit** seals and guarantees (Eph 1:13–14)

If the Spirit's guarantee can fail, then one Person of the Trinity has failed in His assigned role within the economy of salvation. This is theologically untenable.

### C. The Intercession of Christ

> Χριστὸς... ὃς καὶ **ἐντυγχάνει** ὑπὲρ ἡμῶν (Rom 8:34)

Christ's ongoing intercession (present tense ἐντυγχάνει) for believers is perpetual. If a believer for whom Christ intercedes can be lost, then Christ's intercession has failed — contradicting Hebrews 7:25 (παντελὲς σῴζειν δύναται, "He is able to save completely").

### D. The Logical Structure of the ἀρραβών

The commercial metaphor has an internal logic:
1. An ἀρραβών is given by the buyer to guarantee full payment.
2. God has given the Spirit as ἀρραβών.
3. Therefore, God has obligated Himself to complete the "purchase" (ἀπολύτρωσιν τῆς περιποιήσεως, Eph 1:14).
4. If God can fail to complete the purchase, the ἀρραβών was fraudulent.
5. God cannot be fraudulent (Heb 6:18: ἀδύνατον ψεύσασθαι θεόν).
6. Therefore, the completion of salvation is certain for those who have received the ἀρραβών.

## VIII. ADDRESSING THE CONDITIONALIST OBJECTION

The opposing position will argue that salvation is conditional on continued faith. We acknowledge that Scripture commands perseverance (Col 1:23; Heb 3:14). But we distinguish between:

- **The means** of perseverance (faith, obedience) — which are real and necessary
- **The ground** of perseverance (God's power and promise) — which guarantees the means will be supplied

The ἀρραβών does not eliminate the human responsibility to believe; it guarantees that God will sustain that belief in His elect. As 1 Peter 1:5 states, believers are φρουρουμένους **διὰ πίστεως** — guarded *through faith* — but that faith is itself sustained by δυνάμει θεοῦ (God's power).

The warnings in Hebrews (6:4–6; 10:26–31) function not as descriptions of actual regenerate persons losing salvation, but as:
1. Hypothetical warnings that serve as means of perseverance (the warning itself keeps the elect from falling)
2. Descriptions of those who had external participation in covenant blessings without genuine regeneration (cf. 1 John 2:19)

---

## IX. CONCLUSION

The ἀρραβών metaphor, properly understood within its commercial, grammatical, and theological context, does prove unconditional eternal security for the genuinely regenerate. The security is:

- **Grounded** in God's character (He cannot lie or default)
- **Accomplished** by Christ's finished work and ongoing intercession
- **Applied** by the Spirit's sealing and indwelling
- **Expressed** through the believer's persevering faith (which God Himself sustains)

The question is not whether believers must persevere — they must. The question is whether God guarantees that His elect *will* persevere. The ἀρραβών says He does. To deny this is to say that God gives a down-payment He may never honor — making the Almighty a defaulting debtor, which is blasphemous.

> **ὁ δὲ κατεργασάμενος ἡμᾶς εἰς αὐτὸ τοῦτο θεός, ὁ δοὺς ἡμῖν τὸν ἀρραβῶνα τοῦ πνεύματος.** (2 Cor 5:5)

The One who *prepared us for this very thing* is God — the One who *gave us the guarantee* of the Spirit. The preparation and the guarantee are both divine acts. The conclusion is inescapable: what God has guaranteed, God will complete.
